FROM SLUMS TO MANHOLES: THE LIFE OF A SANITARY WORKER IN PAKISTAN

The Constitution of the Islamic Republic of Pakistan is a living document that aims to enshrine important fundamental human rights in Chapter 1. However, the implementation of Chapter 1 has tragically failed with respect to sanitation workers, primarily due to governmental negligence and the courts' inability to effectively uphold these rights. There is a notable absence of a legal framework or government policies designed to provide and safeguard the rights of sanitary workers in Pakistan. Furthermore, the development of jurisprudence ensuring their rights has been minimal, rendering Article 184(3) of the Constitution of the Islamic Republic of Pakistan a mere dead letter for sanitation workers. This article aims to delve into the ways in which specific constitutional rights of sanitary workers have been curtailed, ultimately leading to certain conclusions.

The report published by the National Commission for Human Rights has made it abundantly clear that the majority of individuals undertaking sanitation jobs in our country belong to minority groups. This raises the question: Why do minorities constitute the largest portion of sanitation workers in Pakistan? The answer lies in the lack of access to quality education guaranteed under Article 25A of the Constitution. This deficiency in education opportunities leads to a scarcity of alternative job prospects, thereby leaving sanitation-related positions as one of the few available options.

According to Article 25A of the constitution, it becomes the responsibility of the incumbent government to ensure the education of all citizens of Pakistan between the ages of five and sixteen. Regrettably, the literacy rate among minorities in Pakistan has remained distressingly low for centuries, resulting in thousands being unable to attend schools and colleges. In such circumstances, many are compelled to take up sanitation jobs to financially support their households during periods of economic recession. Consequently, this situation triggers the implications of Article 25 of the constitution, which guarantees the equality of citizens before the law. However, true equality cannot be achieved until these individuals are provided with equitable opportunities. In conclusion, the overrepresentation of minorities among sanitation workers is rooted in the lack of proper education as guaranteed by Article 25A. Addressing this educational disparity is crucial to breaking the cycle that confines these individuals to particular job sectors and is essential to upholding the constitutional principle of equality

There have been instances where vacancies for sanitation workers were available, but the eligibility criteria were limited to minorities of the country. This constitutes a blatant violation of Article 25, resulting in a division between two classes. The founder of Pakistan, Muhammad Ali Jinnah, clearly emphasized the equality of all citizens in his seminal address. However, such discriminatory practices or classifications only serve to create a rift within the underprivileged class.

It's evident that Article 4(2)(a) and Article 9 encapsulate a fundamental human right: the right to life. Sanitation workers, when exposed to manholes, also expose themselves to hazardous gases. Due to the presence of organic matter, when oxygen is lacking, it decomposes into Hydrogen Sulphide, creating a lethal environment within the manhole for these workers. Unfortunately, in Pakistan, workers enter manholes without proper protective gear, resulting in severe skin and health issues. Tragically, this sometimes leads to fatalities, thereby infringing upon the rights enshrined in Article 4(2)(a) and Article 9 and one such incident amidst numerous others occurred in 2022, when two sanitary workers lost their lives in DHA Karachi while performing duties within a manhole.

It is pertinent to note here that the following matter falls to be one of public importance involving fundamental human rights thus paving way for invocation of article 184(3) of the Constitution. A matter becomes of public importance when it directly effects the public at large. The following phrase can be bifurcated into two portions i.e., directly and public at large. The following situation becomes direct as safety kits are not being provided to sanitary workers by the government, leading to deaths of sanitary workers. The second element involves “public at large”, limiting public at large to a specified number of groups would be anomalous but when one talks about public at large it speaks for a significant portion of the citizenry. Here it leaves no doubt that sanitation works are undertaken by minorities of the country, that make a significant and considerable portion of the citizenry. The last element of article 184 (3) requires there to be involvement of fundamental rights, which have been considered in detail above.

It goes without saying that the jurisprudence developed by our courts have been negligible in the aspect. Attention could be drawn towards the case Suo Moto Action Regarding Covid-19 (2020 SCMR 1036) where it was emphasized in an “order” merely that rights of sanitary workers be secured and they be provided with safety kits however, no progress seems to have been made in this regard.

In conclusion,it is important to recognize that solutions can be found for every challenge, including the one we have discussed. A crucial step is to raise widespread awareness about the difficulties faced by sanitation workers. Additionally, the government should take the initiative to compile data on a regional basis concerning these workers. This data can then be utilized to establish a monthly stipend and extend health benefits to them. Equipping sanitary workers with necessary items like masks and protective clothing should also be a priority, as it can significantly reduce health risks. Lastly, ensuring proper training and expertise among those in this profession is essential. By implementing these measures, we can work towards improving the lives and working conditions of sanitation workers, fostering a more equitable and just society for all.
