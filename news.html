Letter to Chief Justice of Pakistan

Honorable Mr. Justice Qazi Faez Isa
 Chief Justice of Supreme Court,
 Republic of Pakistan

Subject: Request for enforcing fundamental rights of sanitary workers in Pakistan under article 184(3) of Constitution of Pakistan,1973

I, Zarrar Haider Bhatti & Ammar Junaid, hope that this letter finds you in the best of health and high spirits. I am writing to you with the utmost respect and trust in the wisdom and integrity of the esteemed office you hold as the Chief Justice of Pakistan.

As a concerned citizen of this nation, I am compelled to highlight an issue that has long been neglected, one that not only involves the violation of constitutional rights but also underscores the state's failure to address it adequately. Over the years, sanitary workers in Pakistan have endured marginalization and discrimination, resulting in a blatant violation of their constitutional rights. The purpose of this letter is to shed light on the hardships faced by these sanitary workers, which have led to severe health and psychological issues. The state's historical silence on their rights and its failure to provide adequate support has prompted me to write this letter. Consequently, I will outline how the constitutional rights of sanitary workers are being violated, necessitating the invocation of Article 184(3) of the constitution.

It is essential to highlight that a significant proportion of sanitary workers in Pakistan belong to minority communities. In a population where 96.5% of people identify as Muslims, and minorities such as Christians and Hindus make up 1.6% and 1.7% respectively, it is striking that approximately 80% of sanitary workers are from minority backgrounds. This stark contrast raises concerns about discriminatory and marginalizing policies implemented by the state.Article 25(1) of the Constitution underscores the principle of equality among all citizens. However, this principle becomes illusory when the state itself, in the allocation of sanitary worker positions, reserves these opportunities exclusively for non-Muslims. One such situation arose This policy not only perpetuates discrimination but also contradicts the spirit of equality enshrined in our constitution.

The constitutional mandate outlined in Article 9 establishes the fundamental right to life, which has evolved into a universally recognized norm in recent times, imposing an unassailable obligation on the state to safeguard it at all costs. Regrettably, the lives of sanitary workers have been tragically extinguished due to the state's failure to provide them with the basic equipment essential for sanitation work and its inadequate provision of rescue services.

One such heart-wrenching incident serves as a poignant example: the untimely demise of Irfan Masih from Umer Kot, who was transported to a hospital only to be left unattended by medical professionals. His tragic fate was sealed as, from the perspective of these doctors, his occupation in sanitation had rendered him impure. Similarly, in the year 2021, Faisal Masih and Nadeem Masih lost their lives while valiantly attempting to rescue Michael Maish when rescue services callously refused to provide assistance to him while he was trapped in a manhole. The perilous nature of manholes, characterized by the presence of noxious gases, creates a deadly environment. When the human body is exposed to these noxious gases, the risk of fatality escalates significantly. Despite this alarming reality, a substantial number of sanitary workers in Pakistan continue to operate within manholes without access to rudimentary protective equipment such as jumpsuits, PVC boots, safety goggles, masks, and safety harnesses. These are fundamental mitigatory measures that must be rigorously enforced by the state, yet their absence perpetuates a breach of the constitutional commitment laid forth in Article 9. It is imperative that immediate attention and corrective action be taken to rectify this situation, ensuring the safety and well-being of sanitary workers who tirelessly contribute to the cleanliness and hygiene of our communities. Failure to do so not only compound the violation of their constitutional rights but also diminishes the state's commitment to upholding the sacred principle of the right to life for all its citizens.

Sanitary workers endure abysmal working conditions that not only expose them to the risk of severe respiratory diseases, as previously mentioned, but also subject their bodies to the perils of handling filthy human and industrial waste. This grim reality not only contributes to respiratory ailments but also inflicts significant skin issues upon these dedicated workers.

Regrettably, in the face of these dire circumstances, there appears to be an absence of a coherent state policy aimed at effectively safeguarding the rights of sanitary workers. It is imperative to note that the Supreme Court, in accordance with Article 184(3) of the Constitution, retains the authority to assume jurisdiction over matters concerning the enforcement of rights enshrined in Chapter 1 of the Constitution.

Continuing to remain silent in the face of these challenges would undoubtedly result in a grievous infringement of the rights of sanitary workers, constituting a direct violation of Article 184(3). Therefore, in light of these compelling circumstances, it is respectfully submitted that the Supreme Court, in fulfillment of its constitutional duty, exercises its original jurisdiction to champion the rights of sanitary workers diligently.

With profound respect and best regards,

Zarrar Haider Bhatti
